the proof · same spectrum, opposite arrow (wiener–khinchin escape)

Up-sweep (blue) and down-sweep (red) are time-reversals of each other, so their power spectra lie exactly on top of one another. A Fourier-magnitude readout sees one curve and cannot tell them apart.

The sign flips because L = Im(zₖ·z̄ₖ(t−lag)) is a bilinear cross-time product — a phase-coupling term, not an energy term. That is the only place direction can live.

what is honest here / what is not

The front end is a filterbank (an FFT grouped into log-spaced bands — the cochlea's job). That part is spectral. The novelty is not in decomposing the sound; it is in the readout.

Verified, live, in front of you: each island is a directed-edge complex cell zₖ = ⟨bandₖ⟩ + i⟨bandₖ₊₁⟩; its angular momentum Lₖ = Im(zₖ(t)·z̄ₖ(t−lag)) = bₖ₊₁(t)·bₖ(t−lag) − bₖ(t)·bₖ₊₁(t−lag) is the signed rotation of energy across that edge. Net L > 0 ⇔ energy sweeping up; < 0 ⇔ down. It flips on time-reversal. The wattage meters are field velocity |ds/dt| and spike rate — both genuinely fall toward zero on steady input.

The ceiling (the honest correction): a magnitude readout √(⟨X,gₛ⟩²+⟨X,gᵧ⟩²) is power-spectrum-equivalent (Wiener–Khinchin) and is blind to direction at matched spectrum. Direction is recoverable only from the bilinear / phase-coupling term — that is exactly why this works and why a Fourier filter cannot. The proof panel shows the matched spectra it cannot use.

Still the bet, untouched: none of this claims the held field is experienced, or that this is "a brain." It is one cheap, event-driven geometric primitive doing a real signal-processing job that magnitude readouts can't do cheaply. That's all it claims — and it's enough to be useful.
